Warm-up Strategies for Achilles Tendon

Achilles Tendon

Suture anchors (SAs) have been prevalently used in orthopedic surgery for repair of rotator cuff tears by inserting in lesser or great tuberosity by tendon-to-bone fixation. Mechanical strength in the bone-screw interface was taken as the primary consideration of SAs designs. Beneath the fixation of suture-tendon interface, under successive removal loading, SAs pullout is the most common mechanism in mechanical failure. In these cases, couples of patient-related and design-related factors involved in mechanical fixation strength against pull-out force could aggravate chronic and symptomatic progression of rotator cuff tear. This study focuses on the relevance between anchor removal and design-related factors including structures and materials.

Fig.1. SAs vary in different materials depending on the clinical purpose. For example, metallic materials, e.g. stainless steel, provide better fixation strength in osteoporotic cases with stronger properties.
Fig.2. To attach the soft tissue to bone surface, SAs are used in 2 subparts: suture is stitched to rupture tendon with strong fixation and the anchors are inserted into bone until the body beneath the surface [1]

Final Product

In this 4-year project, we have developed a whole new SA with distinctive appearance, novel material, and workable mechanical strength. I've learned CAD, finite element analysis, CNC & 3D printing, ASTM F-543 procedure. In addition to these conventional procedures for designing implants, we also conducted biomechanical analysis using robotic arm/UFS system.

Moreover, based on the consideration of reduction of additional cost from "one time" surgical tool in rotator cuff repair, I designed a new one equipped with torque-control mechanism to prevent overloading during clinical treatment and published it into US patent. I was also granted College Student Research Scholarship by Ministration of Science and Technology, Taiwan, for self-managing this design.

In the end, I want to thank all the team who have worked with me in this project for almost 4 years. The final product, a novel magnesium-based suture anchor, is examined by FDA certification now. After these long-term efforts, I sincerely hope someday this product we have designed would help more and more people around the world.
