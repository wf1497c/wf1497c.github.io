Rotator Cuff Repair Biomechanical Test

Rotator Cuff Repair

Supraspinatus, which occupies the supraspinous fossa on the posterior side of scapula, aids deltoid in abduction in arm and reinforces humeral head to hold in the glenoid cavity without slippage during carrying weight. Served as the dominated muscles in abduction of arm, the tendon sometimes gets rupture because of loading with inadequate values or trajectories. Such tear would cause motor pattern deficiencies in rotation and lead to work disability. In the United State, more than 40,000 rotator cuff repair surgeries every year, which indicates the importance of this clinical treatment.
One common surgical treatment is to fix the rupture tendon by using suture anchors (SAs), which is a special bone screw for attaching the soft tissue onto the bone surface. The suture would be used to stitched the tendon and the screws are "anchored" beneath the bone tiisue for fixation. However, there are couple of clinical studies indicate that failure factors in such surgery, including insufficient pullout strength, bone mass quality, suture techniques, and so on. Not all of the surgeries' failures are specifically caused by these factors, as a result, to develop an evaluated system for the whole "fixation" is necessary.

Robotic/UFS System

Inspired by remarkable studies by Woo et al. (1996), in this study, we tried to construct a biomechanical testing system for rotator cuff repair by using robotics/UFS system, which is composed of a robotic arm and an universal force sensor.

The first part of this system is the robotic arm. We chose UR10 (Universal Robots, Odense, Denmark) as our testing machine because of its high accuracy during rotation (less than 0.02 deg). The function of this robotic arm is to simulate common rotatory motions in human shoulder joint. By this manipulator, we can mimic these motions in path-recording function. There are two common modes of this robotic arm to duplicate the motions, position-controlled and force-controlled, but we only used the first one to compare different values of force and moment with the same path because the iterative procedures of the force -controlled seem to be difficult to control within a large scale rotation.

The second part of this system is the universal force sensor which can record forces and moments during duplicated motions by the robotic manipulator. The combination of such two parts enables us to apply the principle of superposition to find the in-situ force in supraspinatus for quantitative evaluations.

Glenohumeral Joint

Glenohumeral joint is formed from the combination of glenoid fossa of the scapula and the humeral head. This joint is a typical ball-and-socket joint with 6 D.O.F. free. The rounded surface of the humeral head moves within glenoid cavity by compression applied from rotator cuff, as a result, if one of the tendons in this group be rupture, the balance of forces here would be broken and cause joint instability. Three common motions in shoulder, which are all single axis rotations, are simulated by the robotic manipulator: abduction, flexion, and lateral rotation. Supraspinatus plays a crucial role in stabilizing the glenohumeral joint during all ranges of motions and initiating abduction, since that, this tendon has been reported the most frequent tearing one in rotator cuff by strenuous circumduction of the arm, shoulder dislocation, or repetitive use of the arm in a position above horizontal and we chose it as our research target.

Tissue Conditions

Five fresh frozen porcine shoulder joints were tested. Porcine shoulder wrapped with saline-soaked gauze and covered with cealed bag under -20oC, and got slowly thawed in room temperature before the dissection. All samples are preprocessed with careful dissection of soft tissue, including other muscles in rotator cuff, and only supraspinatus was remained as intact group. The main reason in this procedure is to reduce cumulated errors in inter-observed mechanical properties of muscles in the whole joint and the interactions between them.

In short, supraspinatus remained state was defined as intact group. After duplicated motions recorded, supraspinatus was dissected and the joint become unbound i.e. no tendon there. Results

Resultant forces and moments sensed by UFS represent functions of the whole fixation, including tendon groups, bone, SAs, and suture. Percentage contribution is an important index to show how much portion of force and moment supraspinatus provides in this joint. All these data is statistical analysed.

In Summary, this study showed a potential way to evaluate soft tissue repair by investigating the loading within the whole fixation. With this procedure, more investigation on implants within animal body can be done under cycling loading conditions.The robotic manipulator can learn and prerecord the very complex motion of these articulating joints, thus enabling the individual study of many tendons from the same specimen.
